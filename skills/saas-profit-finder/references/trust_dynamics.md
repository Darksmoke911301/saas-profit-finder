# Trust Dynamics Reference

The 15 trust dynamics Claude consistently misses when generating SaaS recommendations. Every agent in this skill must reference this file when its work touches buyer behavior, founder positioning, or market access.

## Why This Exists
LLMs treat SaaS like a rational feature-comparison game. Buyers are not rational evaluators. Most B2B sales — especially in boring profitable bootstrappable markets — are won or lost on identity, relationships, and trust signals long before features matter. A great product sold by the wrong person to the wrong buyer with the wrong intro path is a dead product.

These dynamics are not optional considerations. They are buying forces or buying vetoes.

## The 15 Trust Dynamics

### 1. Founder Credibility Tax
Who the founder is determines who'll take their call. Age, accent, perceived social class, online persona, credentials, prior employer, gender, race, regional dialect — these are pricing inputs into every cold conversation. The product can be perfect; if the seller can't get the meeting, the product doesn't exist.

**Implication:** Always ask "would this buyer take this founder seriously in a 5-minute cold call?" If the answer is "probably not" without warm intro, the market is gated by trust, not features.

### 2. In-Group Signaling
Industries have insider markers: vocabulary, conferences attended, mutual acquaintances, certifications, dress code, regional dialect. Outsiders get filtered in 30 seconds.

**Implication:** Founders need either insider status, an insider co-founder, or an insider mentor/advisor who lends credibility. Without one of those, vertical SaaS in tight industries (legal, medical, faith, trades, finance, agriculture) is extremely hard.

### 3. Expert-as-Product Domains
Personal trainers, therapists, lawyers, doctors, financial advisors, real estate agents, hairstylists, designers, consultants. Their value is tacit knowledge of their specific client. **They ARE the product.**

**Implication:** SaaS targeting these professionals must augment their expertise, not appear to replace it. Generic recommendations ("3 sets of 10 squats") insult them. Memory of their specific clients ("Sarah's knee flared up after the ski trip") is valuable. AI that says "I know your client better than you" gets rejected.

### 4. Buyer Status Anxiety
B2B buyers fear looking stupid. They won't buy products that imply they need help with their core job. They'll buy products that make them look like innovators.

**Implication:** Positioning matters more than features. "AI co-pilot for sales reps" implies the reps need help — they'll resist. "AI tool that helps your team win bigger deals" implies the manager is driving innovation — they buy.

### 5. The Trust Radius Limit
Some markets are cold-email-immune. HOA boards, school administrators, healthcare practices, family-owned businesses, faith-based orgs, mid-market legal/finance, government. They buy only through warm intro or known reputation.

**Implication:** If founder's distribution plan for these markets is "cold email," the plan is wrong. The plan should be "find a warm intro path" or pick a different market.

### 6. Tacit Knowledge > Data
In many domains, operator tacit knowledge dominates any dataset. Trainers know their client's marriage is falling apart. Sales reps know which decision-maker is faking authority. Designers know which feedback to ignore.

**Implication:** Products that ignore tacit knowledge feel irrelevant. Products that capture and amplify tacit knowledge feel like magic. Build for the second pattern.

### 7. Professional Pride / Threat Detection
A 30-year HVAC dispatcher doesn't want "AI to optimize routes." Even if it works better. It tells them their judgment is replaceable.

**Implication:** Products that read as "you're now redundant" face resistance from the user even when the buyer approves. Sell to the user's pride: "tool that makes your judgment more valuable" instead of "tool that replaces your judgment."

### 8. Personal Liability Fear
Decision-makers in regulated industries (healthcare, legal, finance, K-12) carry personal liability. Software that increases exposure won't be bought regardless of features. Software that decreases exposure sells itself.

**Implication:** In regulated markets, frame the product as liability reduction. "Avoid the $50k mistake" beats "save 10 hours/week."

### 9. Switching Costs Are Relational, Not Technical
Why do small businesses stay on terrible software? Because they have a relationship with the rep, or someone internal championed it. Replacing the software means a relationship break.

**Implication:** Don't model switching costs as data migration effort. Model them as interpersonal disruption. Provide cover stories the customer can use to switch ("the new tool integrates with our compliance partner") so the switch isn't a personal betrayal.

### 10. Authority Transfer
B2B SaaS sells through implicit authority transfer — buyers adopt tools that respected peers use. With no peers using it, the product is dead.

**Implication:** Early customers are not just revenue. They are reputation collateral. Pick first customers who will become reference customers for the rest of the market — even if they're harder to close.

### 11. Decision-Maker vs Influencer Mismatch
Often the user wants the product but the buyer doesn't care. Or vice versa. Office managers want better software; owners sign checks based on golf-course conversations.

**Implication:** Map the buying committee. Identify who feels the pain, who has the budget, who has veto power. Sell to all three with different framings.

### 12. Generation Mismatch as Hard Friction
A 60-year-old practice manager won't learn a new SaaS that requires workflow changes. They'll outlast the founder.

**Implication:** Selling to entrenched legacy operators is harder than it looks. Either find the rare innovator, or target the next-generation operators inheriting the business, or target operators with a forcing function (compliance, retirement, ownership change).

### 13. Reputation Compounds, Not Features
Trust-dependent verticals reward incumbents that have been around 10+ years. New entrants can't shortcut this.

**Implication:** In reputation-heavy markets, plan for 18-24 month reputation-building phase. If founder needs revenue in 6 months, pick a different market.

### 14. The "Why Should I Trust You" Default
Cold buyers assume founders are scammers until proven otherwise. Proof requires: known mutual contact, credible employer history, industry certification, real address, phone number that's a real human, OR a track record.

**Implication:** Audit founder's public trust signals. LinkedIn presence, prior credible employers, mutual connections to first 10 prospects, real address visible, real phone. Cold outreach without these is shouting into the void.

### 15. Cultural / Geographic / Demographic Mismatch
Selling to demographic A from demographic B has cost. Urban founder → rural HVAC owner: friction. White-collar founder → trades: friction. Young founder → older buyer: friction. These compound with everything else.

**Implication:** Score founder-buyer demographic match honestly. If mismatch is severe, either: (a) pick a different market, (b) hire/partner with someone who fits, (c) commit to a longer ramp to build credibility.

## Trust Audit Checklist (for every opportunity)

Before scoring any idea, answer all 15:

1. **Founder Credibility Tax**: Would this buyer take this specific founder's cold call seriously? Y/N + why.
2. **In-Group Signaling**: Does the founder have insider status, an insider co-founder/advisor, or none? If none, what's the credibility-borrowing plan?
3. **Expert-as-Product**: Is the buyer selling their own expertise? If yes, does the product augment or threaten that expertise? Specifically how.
4. **Buyer Status Anxiety**: Does the positioning make the buyer look like an innovator or a slacker? Reframe if needed.
5. **Trust Radius**: Is this market cold-email-receptive or warm-intro-only? Match distribution plan accordingly.
6. **Tacit Knowledge**: Does the product capture tacit knowledge, or assume the data tells the full story? Specify which.
7. **Professional Pride**: Does the user (not just buyer) feel threatened or empowered? Provide concrete reasoning.
8. **Personal Liability**: Is there a liability angle to lean into or avoid? Frame accordingly.
9. **Relational Switching Costs**: Who internally championed the incumbent? What's the cover story for switching?
10. **Authority Transfer**: Who are the 3-5 reference customers that, once landed, unlock the rest of the market?
11. **Buying Committee**: User, budget holder, veto-haver. Name all three for the target ICP.
12. **Generation Match**: What's the age/tenure of the buyer? Are they innovators, default operators, or legacy holdouts?
13. **Reputation Timeline**: Realistic months to build enough reputation for inbound demand. Match against founder's runway.
14. **Founder Trust Signals**: What does the founder's public footprint show? Cite specific gaps.
15. **Demographic Match**: Founder demographics vs buyer demographics. Score friction 1-5.

## Red-Flag Patterns

The opportunity is likely trust-broken if any apply:

- Founder is 18-30 selling to buyers who are 50+ in a status-conscious vertical, with no warm intros.
- Founder targets a tight community (faith, trades, ethnic-cultural niche) without insider status.
- Product replaces expertise that the buyer's identity is built on.
- Distribution plan is cold email to a known warm-intro-only market.
- Founder has no public trust signals (LinkedIn, prior employer, real address).
- First-10-customers plan doesn't name anyone in the founder's actual network.
- Founder's prior product attempt in this market failed for trust reasons (audit honestly).

If 2+ red flags apply, the trust audit should DOWNGRADE the opportunity regardless of how high the feature/market score is.
